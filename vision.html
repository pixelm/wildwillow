wildwillow

Working document · June 2026

Vision

Zocdoc for alternative healing practitioners.

Alternative healing in America is fragmented. Practitioners — acupuncturists, Ayurvedic doctors, naturopaths, clinical herbalists, yoga therapists, integrative MDs — are forced to be both healers and small business owners. Most are bad at the business side and resent it. Meanwhile, patients who could be helped by these practitioners can't find them — they Google their symptoms, end up on content farms or supplement marketplaces, and default to conventional medicine because no one shows them what else exists.

Wildwillow becomes the platform that solves both sides. Lead-gen for practitioners, admin layer for their practice, and an editorial brand that earns parents' trust as the credible entry point into alternative healing.

The problem

Two structural failures, one platform.

Demand side. Parents searching for natural remedies for their kids land on listicles with no editorial judgment, supplement marketers selling whatever has the highest margin, or generic AI wellness content. The qualified practitioners are invisible to those searches. So parents default to conventional medicine and prescriptions because they don't know what else exists. Many would explore alternatives if someone made the introduction easy and credible.

Supply side. Alternative healing practitioners are fragmented and undersupported. They run their own websites, handle their own intake, do their own marketing, manage their own scheduling and billing. No platform serves them the way Headway serves mental health providers or Zocdoc serves MDs. Existing tools (Mindbody, Pabau) are built for fitness studios and aesthetic clinics — not for traditional medicine practices.

Anyone who cares about traditional medicine reaching the families who need it has been waiting for someone to build this properly.

What we do

Two layers. We're building them in order.

1

Lead-gen and education

A curated content site organized by condition across four modalities: Ayurveda, Western herbalism, yoga, breathwork. Each page shows where the traditions agree and disagree, what to try first at home (over-the-counter products plus practices), what to skip, and — critically — when home remedies aren't enough and a practitioner is the right next step. The free editorial product is the customer acquisition engine; a paid AI agent personalizes the content to each parent's specific situation. See the prototype.

2

Practitioner platform

Local practitioners listed on each condition page, with availability, reviews, and booking for telehealth or in-person visits. Lead-gen revenue first — practitioners pay to be listed. Over time, the platform takes on admin work for them: incoming requests, scheduling, communication, payment, treatment plan management, patient tracking. This is the sticky revenue. Once a practitioner runs their practice on wildwillow, they don't leave.

The order matters. Content gets traffic. Traffic gives practitioners a reason to list. Listings make the platform valuable enough to take over admin. Each layer makes the next more valuable.

The market

Headline numbers for "complementary and alternative medicine" are unreliable — US estimates range from $29B to $70B for 2024-2025 depending on who you ask. The numbers that matter are practitioner counts and comparable platform economics.

The practitioner base. Roughly 60,000-70,000 core alternative practitioners in the US — acupuncturists (~40,000), naturopaths (~7,000), Ayurvedic practitioners (~3,000), integrative MDs (~4,000), clinical herbalists (~2,500), yoga therapists (~5,500), functional medicine practitioners (~4,000). Plus ~400,000 chiropractors and massage therapists if we expand to adjacent groups in year 3+.

The consumer base. About 36% of US adults use complementary health approaches in a given year — roughly 95 million people. For our entry point (parents seeking alternative care for kids): ~16-19 million addressable parents.

The platform comp. Headway is the right reference. They serve 70,000 mental health providers, reached that scale in ~5 years from founding, currently valued around $2.3B. The practitioner base and the trajectory are directly analogous to what wildwillow becomes for alternative healing.

Risks

Clinical liability.

AI generating health recommendations sits in regulatory gray space. Mitigation: human-in-the-loop review of generated content before it's promoted to public cache, conservative defaults that refer to practitioners earlier rather than later, proper liability insurance from launch, and tight scope (children's wellness, not acute illness) that stays clearly on the wellness side of the FDA's general-wellness exemption.

Agent quality at scale.

The agent has to be good enough that parents trust it across enough query variations to be useful. Mitigation: tight scope (children's conditions only, four modalities only), aggressive editorial review for the first 100 pages before generation goes more autonomous, your clinical oversight as the quality bar.

Marketplace cold-start.

Two-sided marketplaces are notoriously hard to bootstrap. Mitigation: starting in Austin with practitioners you already know via IPA, the content layer brings demand for free, and slow supply growth matched to demand growth rather than launching with 200 listings on day one.

Competition from well-funded entrants.

A big telehealth company — Headway expanding, Zocdoc going alternative — could enter this space. Mitigation: editorial moat takes years to build, the four-modality scope keeps us moving faster than a general-purpose competitor, practitioner relationships once built are sticky, and the children's wellness positioning is a vertical big platforms would consider niche.
